Gender Gap in Math Efficacy

Description

Interaction: Click any country in the left dumbbell chart to see that country's detailed gender breakdown (Female vs Male efficacy scores) displayed in the right bar chart.

This PISA 2022 visualization uses a dumbbell chart to show gender gaps in math self-efficacy across the top 20 countries with the largest gaps. Each horizontal line connects the Female (pink) and Male (blue) efficacy scores for a country.

Math self-efficacy measures students' confidence in their mathematical abilities. Countries with longer lines between points have larger gender gaps. Click a country to compare specific Female and Male scores in the driven chart.

Math Anxiety vs Performance

Description

Interaction: Click any cell in the heatmap to see the gender distribution (Female vs Male student counts) for that specific combination of anxiety level and math performance level.

This PISA 2022 heatmap displays the relationship between math anxiety (Y-axis, from Very Low to Very High) and math performance levels (X-axis, Levels 1-5). Darker cells indicate higher student counts in that combination.

The inverse relationship between anxiety and performance is evident: students with "Very Low" anxiety tend to cluster at higher performance levels (4-5), while "Very High" anxiety students concentrate at lower levels (1-2). Click cells to explore gender patterns within each anxiety-performance combination.

Description

Parent Education Effect on Math

Description

Interaction: Click a parent education level in the left grouped bar chart to see the comparison of that education level's effect across all three data sources (HSLS US, PISA US, PISA International) in the right chart.

Parent education levels have been harmonized across datasets into five comparable categories: Less than HS, HS Diploma, Some College, Bachelor's, and Graduate+. This allows direct comparison of the "parent education premium" across sources.

The visualization reveals whether the advantage of having more educated parents is similar in the US (across time) and internationally, or whether the US educational system amplifies or dampens the effect of family educational background.
