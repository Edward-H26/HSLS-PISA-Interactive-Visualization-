Introduction

About This Dashboard

This interactive dashboard explores factors that influence educational equity and STEM career pathways through analysis of two complementary data sources. The Programme for International Student Assessment (PISA) 2022 provides cross national comparisons of 15 year old students across 81 participating countries and economies, while the High School Longitudinal Study of 2009 (HSLS:09) offers longitudinal tracking of over 23,000 U.S. students from ninth grade through their postsecondary years and early careers. Together, these datasets enable examination of how family background, socioeconomic status, and demographic characteristics shape academic outcomes and career aspirations in science, technology, engineering, and mathematics fields.

Through nine interactive visualizations organized into three thematic sections, the dashboard enables users to examine how parental education levels, household resources, and geographic context relate to students' academic performance and STEM career expectations. Users can explore how structural inequalities and individual level factors jointly shape educational outcomes across different demographic groups, national contexts, and time periods. The combination of international cross sectional data with U.S. longitudinal data provides insights into both the universality and cultural specificity of educational stratification patterns.

The longitudinal design of HSLS:09 provides a unique opportunity to track how early attitudes and experiences in mathematics and science relate to subsequent educational choices and career outcomes. This approach offers insights that cross sectional studies cannot capture. Because the study follows the same students over an eight year period from 2009 to 2017, it reveals patterns of continuity and change in academic performance and career expectations that illuminate both the persistence of educational inequalities and the points at which interventions might be most effective. The PISA 2022 data complements this longitudinal perspective by enabling comparisons across educational systems with different policies, cultural values, and economic conditions.

Research Questions

This dashboard addresses several interconnected research questions about educational equity and STEM participation. A major concern is the influence of parental education on academic achievement and career aspirations. The analysis examines whether students with more highly educated parents maintain higher GPAs throughout high school and how the magnitude of these gaps changes from 9th through 12th grade. The dashboard also investigates how parental education relates to family income and whether STEM career expectations differ by geographic locale and gender.

The dashboard examines the role of household resources in shaping educational outcomes. It explores how access to information and communication technology resources relates to academic performance and whether these relationships differ by immigration status and socioeconomic background. The analysis considers how digital divides intersect with other dimensions of educational inequality to produce cumulative advantages or disadvantages for different student populations.

Beyond individual and family level factors, the dashboard examines geographic and demographic disparities in STEM outcomes. It compares STEM major enrollment rates across U.S. Census regions to determine whether regional patterns exist in who pursues STEM fields. The analysis also considers how race and ethnicity relate to academic trajectories and investigates patterns of GPA change across different racial and ethnic groups to understand whether achievement gaps widen, narrow, or remain stable over the high school years.

The dashboard investigates psychological factors that mediate the relationship between background characteristics and academic outcomes. It examines how mathematics anxiety relates to school belonging and academic performance and whether these relationships vary by gender and parental education level. The analysis explores how mathematics self efficacy and persistence relate to immigration status and whether students from immigrant families show different patterns of academic motivation compared to native born peers.

These questions reveal the intersecting inequalities that shape educational pathways, specifically how family background factors combine with psychological characteristics and contextual influences to determine STEM outcomes. Through examination of these relationships together, the dashboard aims to identify which factors appear most strongly associated with STEM career expectations and how multiple dimensions of advantage or disadvantage combine to influence educational trajectories.

Conclusion

Synthesis of Findings

The nine linked visualizations in this dashboard reveal consistent patterns of educational stratification by family background, with notable variations across geographic regions, demographic groups, and national contexts. These findings address the research questions posed in the Introduction, specifically examining how parental education shapes academic trajectories, whether achievement gaps widen or narrow over time, how family background combines with psychological factors to influence STEM outcomes, and which factors appear most strongly associated with STEM career expectations. The analysis draws on sociological theories of stratification, including Bourdieu's cultural capital framework, the Wisconsin model of status attainment, and theories of cumulative advantage to explain why these patterns emerge and persist.

The convergence of findings across two distinct data sources strengthens the conclusions presented here. The PISA 2022 cross sectional data, capturing achievement and attitudes among 15 year old students across 81 countries, reveal the contemporaneous associations between family background and educational outcomes in diverse national contexts. The HSLS:09 longitudinal data, tracking over 23,000 U.S. students from ninth grade through postsecondary enrollment, demonstrate how early indicators translate to later outcomes through developmental processes that unfold over eight years. Together, these complementary perspectives illuminate both the universality of educational stratification and its context dependent manifestations.

The Parental Education Gradient

Parental education, measured through X1PAREDU in HSLS (a seven level scale from less than high school through doctoral or professional degree) and HISCED in PISA (the International Standard Classification of Education), emerges as the most consistent predictor of student outcomes across all nine visualizations. This finding aligns with Bourdieu's theory of cultural capital, which posits that highly educated parents transmit advantages to their children through three interconnected mechanisms. First, they provide economic capital, as Visualization 1 demonstrates through the strong positive correlation between parental education and family income (X1FAMINCOME), with doctoral degree holders reporting average incomes approximately five to six times higher than families where parents lack a high school diploma. Second, they transmit cultural capital in the form of educational expectations, academic knowledge, and familiarity with institutional norms. Third, they activate social capital through professional networks that provide information about educational and career opportunities.

The Wisconsin model of status attainment provides a complementary framework for understanding why parental education matters. This model proposes a causal chain linking family background to educational and occupational attainment through the mediating role of aspirations. Visualization 1 directly illustrates this process: within each parental education category, students with the highest educational aspirations (X1STUEDEXPCT coded as expecting graduate or professional degrees) consistently come from families with the highest incomes. Children observe their parents' educational trajectories and calibrate their own expectations accordingly. Those from highly educated families encounter messaging that frames graduate education as a normal and achievable life trajectory, while students from less educated families may perceive greater barriers to advanced education including direct costs, opportunity costs, and cultural distance from academic institutions.

Visualization 7 reveals that parental education also shapes the affective dimensions of students' educational experiences through what might be termed psychological capital transmission. Students whose parents hold bachelor's degrees or higher report significantly lower mathematics anxiety (ANXMAT) than students whose parents did not complete secondary education. This pattern likely reflects multiple processes: more educated parents may model positive attitudes toward mathematics, provide homework assistance that reduces frustration, and communicate confidence in their children's academic abilities. The socioeconomic status variable (ESCS in PISA, X1SES in HSLS), which combines parental education with occupational status and home possessions, shows similarly strong gradients in anxiety levels. Students from low SES backgrounds face what researchers have termed a "double burden" of reduced cognitive resources and elevated affective challenges.

Digital Resources and Immigration Status

Visualizations 2 and 6 examine how access to information and communication technology resources relates to academic performance, addressing the research question of whether digital divides intersect with other dimensions of educational inequality. The ICT resources index (ICTRES) in PISA captures the availability of computers, internet connectivity, and educational software in the home. The home possessions index (HOMEPOS) provides a broader measure of household wealth including both technology and other durables. Both indices show strong positive associations with academic achievement, but the critical finding is that these relationships differ substantially by immigration status.

The PISA immigration status variable (IMMIG) classifies students into three categories based on their own and their parents' birthplaces: native students (born in the country of assessment with at least one parent also born there), second generation immigrants (born in the country with both parents born abroad), and first generation immigrants (both student and parents born abroad). Visualization 2 reveals that native students show the strongest resource to achievement correlations, while first generation immigrants demonstrate considerable heterogeneity in how household resources translate to academic outcomes. This pattern supports segmented assimilation theory, which proposes that immigrant incorporation follows divergent pathways depending on the context of reception, human capital, and social networks available to different immigrant groups.

The theoretical explanation for why resource access alone does not determine achievement centers on the concept of resource conversion. Economic and material resources must be converted into academic capital through processes that require cultural knowledge, time, and supportive environments. First generation immigrant families may possess material resources but lack familiarity with the host country's educational system, limiting their ability to leverage those resources effectively. They may also face linguistic barriers, documentation challenges, and discrimination that constrain resource conversion. Second generation immigrants typically show stronger resource to achievement correlations because they combine parental resources with native born cultural knowledge and English language proficiency. Visualization 6 extends this analysis by comparing ICT usage patterns between OECD and non OECD countries, revealing that technology access is a necessary but not sufficient condition for STEM interest development.

Gender Patterns in Achievement and Confidence

Visualizations 3, 5, and 8 address the research questions about gender gaps in mathematics and reading achievement and their variation across countries and regions. The gender variable (ST004D01T in PISA, X1SEX in HSLS, coded as 1 for female and 2 for male) enables disaggregation of all outcome measures by sex. The findings reveal a consistent but complex pattern: males outperform females in mathematics (PV1MATH) while females substantially outperform males in reading (PV1READ), with science (PV1SCIE) showing more variable patterns depending on national context.

Expectancy value theory, developed by Jacquelynne Eccles and colleagues, provides the most comprehensive framework for understanding these gender differences. The theory proposes that achievement choices and performance depend on individuals' expectations for success and the subjective value they place on different tasks. Gender socialization processes systematically shape both components: girls and boys receive different messages about their abilities in mathematics and literacy domains, leading to divergent expectancies, and cultural stereotypes associate mathematics with masculinity and reading with femininity, creating differential task values. Visualization 3 shows that gender gaps in mathematics persist across all levels of internet usage intensity, suggesting that technology exposure does not eliminate gender differentiated socialization.

A critical finding from Visualization 5 is that gender differences in mathematics self efficacy (MATHEFF) often exceed gender differences in actual mathematics performance. Female students report lower confidence in their mathematical abilities even when they perform comparably to males on standardized assessments. This confidence gap has important consequences because self efficacy influences persistence, course taking, and career aspirations. Stereotype threat theory, developed by Claude Steele and Joshua Aronson, explains part of this phenomenon: awareness of negative stereotypes about one's group can impair performance and undermine confidence, particularly in high stakes testing situations. Female students may internalize societal messages that mathematics is not "for them," leading to reduced self efficacy regardless of actual competence.

Visualization 8 reveals substantial regional variation in gender gaps across academic domains, with some continents showing near parity in mathematics while others demonstrate large male advantages. This variation is theoretically important because it demonstrates that gender gaps are not biologically determined but rather reflect cultural and institutional factors that differ across educational systems. Countries with more gender egalitarian norms and policies tend to show smaller mathematics gaps, supporting the argument that structural interventions can reduce gender stratification in STEM fields.

Regional Variation in STEM Pathways

Visualization 4 addresses the research question of whether regional patterns exist in who pursues STEM fields by mapping STEM major enrollment (X4RFDGMJSTEM, a binary indicator from the 2016 HSLS follow up) across U.S. Census regions. The finding that the South produces the largest absolute number of STEM majors, followed by the Midwest, Northeast, and West, reflects population distribution but also regional economic structures and educational infrastructure. Charles Tilly's concept of opportunity hoarding provides a theoretical framework for understanding these patterns: advantaged groups and regions concentrate resources and opportunities in ways that perpetuate spatial inequality.

The geographic clustering of STEM industries and research institutions creates self reinforcing advantages. Regions with strong STEM labor markets provide students with more visible STEM career paths, more opportunities for internships and mentorship, and stronger economic incentives to pursue STEM education. The school locale variable in HSLS (X1LOCALE, categorizing schools as city, suburb, town, or rural) captures within region variation: Visualization 1 shows that students in city and suburban schools report somewhat higher STEM career expectations (X1STEMOCC, measuring whether students expect to work in a STEM occupation at age 30) compared to town and rural peers. Rural students may encounter fewer STEM professionals in their communities, have less access to advanced mathematics and science coursework, and perceive greater geographic barriers to STEM careers that typically cluster in metropolitan areas.

Visualization 8 extends this analysis to the international level by comparing mathematics achievement across continental regions. The substantial regional variation, with some regions averaging 450 points on the PISA scale while others exceed 500, reflects differences in educational systems, economic development, and cultural attitudes toward education. These patterns underscore that educational stratification operates at multiple geographic scales, from neighborhood and school effects through regional labor markets to national educational policies.

Race, Ethnicity, and Academic Trajectories

Visualization 4 directly addresses the research question of how race and ethnicity relate to academic trajectories through the GPA progression chart. The race/ethnicity composite variable (X1RACE) in HSLS classifies students into eight categories: American Indian or Alaska Native, Asian, Black or African American, Hispanic (race not specified), Hispanic (race specified), more than one race, Native Hawaiian or Pacific Islander, and White. The grade point average variables (X3TGPA9TH, X3TGPA10TH, X3TGPA11TH, X3TGPA12TH) track academic performance across all four high school years on a standard 0 to 4.0 scale.

The findings reveal that achievement gaps present at ninth grade do not remain static but rather show different trajectories across racial and ethnic groups. Asian students maintain the highest average GPAs throughout high school, a pattern consistent with cultural explanations emphasizing educational achievement values and with structural explanations pointing to selective immigration and higher parental education levels among Asian American families. White students show the second highest GPAs with gradual improvement across grades. Black, Hispanic, and American Indian students begin ninth grade with lower average GPAs but show improvement through twelfth grade, with American Indian and Alaska Native students demonstrating the largest gains.

The theory of cumulative advantage and disadvantage, articulated by sociologists Thomas DiPrete and Gregory Eirich, predicts that small initial differences should compound over time as those with early advantages accumulate additional resources while those with early disadvantages fall further behind. The finding that achievement gaps narrow rather than widen during high school suggests that countervailing forces may be at work. School based interventions, maturation effects, and the dropout of lowest performing students may all contribute to narrowing gaps in the observed data. However, the persistence of substantial gaps even at twelfth grade indicates that structural barriers related to school funding, teacher quality, curricular tracking, and neighborhood segregation continue to reproduce racial inequality in educational outcomes.

Psychological Factors and Academic Success

Visualizations 5, 7, and 9 examine the psychological factors that mediate the relationship between background characteristics and academic outcomes, addressing the research questions about mathematics anxiety, school belonging, and self efficacy. These constructs are measured through validated indices in PISA: mathematics anxiety (ANXMAT) captures worry and nervousness about mathematics performance, school belonging (BELONG) measures students' sense of connection to their school community, and mathematics self efficacy (MATHEFF) assesses confidence in the ability to solve various types of mathematics problems. All indices are scaled as weighted likelihood estimates with an OECD average of zero and standard deviation of one.

Albert Bandura's self efficacy theory provides the primary framework for understanding why these psychological factors matter. Self efficacy beliefs influence the choices people make, the effort they expend, their persistence in the face of difficulty, and their emotional reactions to challenging situations. Students with high mathematics self efficacy choose more challenging courses, persist longer on difficult problems, experience less anxiety, and ultimately achieve at higher levels. Visualization 9 demonstrates the positive correlation between mathematics self efficacy and mathematics persistence (MATHPERS, measuring determination and effort in mathematics), showing that these motivational constructs reinforce each other over time.

Visualization 7 reveals that mathematics anxiety varies systematically by parental education and socioeconomic status. Students from more advantaged backgrounds report lower anxiety levels, likely because they have access to more academic support, experience less financial stress at home, and benefit from parental modeling of positive attitudes toward mathematics. The relationship between school belonging and anxiety shows that students who feel connected to their school community report less mathematics anxiety regardless of background, suggesting that belonging may serve as a protective factor that buffers against the negative effects of disadvantage. This finding has important implications for intervention design: schools can potentially reduce anxiety disparities by fostering inclusive environments where all students feel welcomed and valued.

Visualization 9 extends this analysis by examining how immigration status moderates the relationship between psychological factors and achievement. First generation immigrant students show lower average levels of both mathematics self efficacy and persistence compared to native peers, clustering in the lower left quadrant of the efficacy persistence scatter plot. This pattern likely reflects the compounded challenges facing recent immigrants including language barriers, cultural adjustment, and in some cases traumatic experiences. However, the positive correlation between efficacy and persistence is similar across immigration groups, suggesting that interventions that successfully build confidence should produce similar motivational benefits regardless of nativity.

Cross National and Longitudinal Perspectives

The combination of PISA cross sectional data with HSLS longitudinal data provides complementary perspectives that neither data source could offer alone. The PISA assessment captures achievement and attitudes at a single point in time (age 15) across 81 countries and economies, enabling examination of how socioeconomic gradients vary across educational systems with different policies, tracking structures, and cultural contexts. The international comparative design reveals which patterns are universal, appearing across all or most countries, and which are context dependent, varying substantially by national setting. The finding that parental education gradients appear in all countries but vary in magnitude suggests that stratification processes are fundamental to educational systems but amenable to policy intervention.

The HSLS longitudinal design tracks a single cohort of U.S. ninth graders through high school completion (2009 to 2013), postsecondary entry (2013 update), and early career outcomes (2016 follow up). This eight year window enables examination of developmental processes that unfold over time, addressing research questions that cross sectional data cannot answer. Specifically, the HSLS data reveal whether early attitudes and experiences predict later outcomes when background characteristics are held constant. The first visualization demonstrates this predictive power: ninth grade STEM career expectations (X1STEMOCC) show a substantial correlation with actual STEM major enrollment (X4RFDGMJSTEM) seven years later, even controlling for parental education and other background factors. This finding supports the Wisconsin model's emphasis on aspirations as mediating mechanisms linking family background to attainment.

Together, the two data sources demonstrate that educational stratification operates through both contemporaneous associations and developmental processes. The PISA data show that at any given moment, students from advantaged backgrounds outperform their less advantaged peers in ways that reflect ongoing resource disparities, socialization patterns, and institutional contexts. The HSLS data show that these contemporaneous associations translate into long term outcome differences through pathways involving academic preparation, course taking, institutional selection, and credential attainment. Understanding both the synchronic and diachronic dimensions of stratification is essential for designing effective interventions.

Implications for Policy and Practice

The interconnected nature of these findings carries several implications for educational policy and practice. First, the multiple mechanisms linking family background to educational outcomes suggest that single factor interventions may prove insufficient. Providing technology access without addressing confidence and belonging, or building confidence without ensuring adequate academic preparation, may fail to produce lasting improvements. Effective approaches likely require attention to economic, cultural, social, and psychological factors simultaneously. Multi component interventions that combine resource provision, academic support, confidence building, and community connection show the greatest promise based on the patterns revealed in these visualizations.

Second, the substantial regional and national variation in stratification patterns indicates that local context matters for intervention design. Policies that prove effective in one educational system may require adaptation for others. The finding that gender gaps vary dramatically across countries suggests that culturally specific interventions may be more effective than universal approaches. Similarly, the geographic variation in U.S. STEM outcomes implies that rural schools may need different strategies than urban schools, and that regional economic contexts shape the feasibility and appeal of different educational pathways.

Third, the finding that psychological factors mediate the relationship between background and achievement points toward actionable intervention targets. Schools cannot easily change students' family backgrounds, but they can work to reduce mathematics anxiety through supportive instructional practices, foster school belonging through inclusive climate initiatives, and build self efficacy through appropriately challenging tasks and mastery experiences. The evidence that achievement gaps narrow during high school provides grounds for optimism: educational experiences during the secondary years have demonstrated potential to attenuate initial inequalities. Realizing this potential requires targeted support for students from less advantaged backgrounds, attention to the affective as well as cognitive dimensions of learning, and recognition that opportunity structures shape both aspirations and attainments.

Finally, the longitudinal evidence from HSLS demonstrates that early indicators, including ninth grade STEM career expectations and mathematics attitudes, predict outcomes seven years later. This finding underscores the importance of early intervention while also indicating that meaningful change remains possible throughout the educational trajectory. Students who develop interest and confidence in STEM during high school can pursue STEM majors even if their ninth grade profiles did not initially suggest STEM pathways. The dashboard's interactive design enables exploration of these conditional relationships, inviting users to examine how different starting conditions associate with different outcomes and where intervention might prove most effective.

Related Academic Literature and Government Collection

Several major academic and government publications demonstrate the methodologies in HSLS:09. The core technical documentation consists of three primary reports that describe the study's design and implementation across multiple data collection waves. The base year report outlines the initial data collection procedures, sampling strategy, and construction of baseline variables. This document is available at https://nces.ed.gov/pubs2012/2012001.pdf, which offers researchers more context for understanding how the study was planned and executed.

The first follow up report extends the study from the spring 2012 data collection when most participants were in their junior year of high school. This report is available at https://nces.ed.gov/pubs2014/2014361.pdf, which illustrates revisions of the survey instruments, current tracking procedures, and the creation of longitudinal variables that span the first three years of the study.

The most comprehensive documentation covers the period from the base year to the second follow up year. This documentation is available at https://nces.ed.gov/pubs2018/2018140.pdf, which delineates the study design in detail. It is the primary reference for researchers working with data with the second follow up wave. The documentation describes sampling procedures and the construction of weights by introducing the full survey instruments, annotating variable coding schemes and missing data, and discussing nonresponse analyses with weighting adjustments. Hence, this documentation provides variance estimation guidance for complex survey analysis and indicates the public use version and the restricted version.

Since the initial release of HSLS:09, the scholarly literature that utilizes these data has grown substantially in the field of education as well as adjacent fields such as sociology, economics, psychology, and public policy. Researchers have used HSLS:09 to identify typologies of high schools based on their orientation toward STEM, showing how latent classes are associated with school demographics and students' postsecondary enrollment and STEM degree intentions. Research syntheses of dissertations that analyzed HSLS:09 algebra achievement data have demonstrated that student, teacher, parent, and school level factors, including mathematics identity and self-efficacy, are strongly related to 9th and 11th grade algebra outcomes. Studies using the nationally representative HSLS:09 base-year sample of ninth graders have examined how familial, school, and psychological characteristics shape underrepresented students' aspirations for STEM careers, and have profiled which demographic groups of high school students report STEM career aspirations, highlighting systematic differences by gender and race/ethnicity. Collectively, these studies illustrate that HSLS:09 has become a central data source for rigorous scholarly work on high school students' mathematics achievement, educational experiences, and STEM-related pathways.

Background Information about the Dataset

List the Organization from which the Dataset Came From

The Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) is an intergovernmental organization founded in 1961 with 38 member countries, headquartered in Paris, France. The OECD's mission is to promote policies that improve the economic and social well-being of people around the world. In the field of education, the OECD provides comparative international data and analysis through various programs, with PISA being one of its flagship educational assessments.

PISA (Programme for International Student Assessment) was first administered in 2000 and has been conducted every three years since then, with 2022 marking the eighth cycle of the assessment. The program is designed to evaluate educational systems worldwide by testing the skills and knowledge of 15-year-old students in reading, mathematics, and science. Unlike curriculum-based assessments, PISA focuses on measuring students' ability to apply their knowledge to real-life situations, reflecting a competency-based approach to education.

Researchers can access PISA data and documentation through several official channels. The main PISA website is located at https://www.oecd.org/pisa/, which provides comprehensive information about the program, methodology, and findings. Description

This visualization presents two linked charts analyzing the relationship between parental education, family socioeconomic status, student educational aspirations, and STEM career expectations. The data originates from the High School Longitudinal Study of 2009 (HSLS:09), a nationally representative longitudinal study conducted by the National Center for Education Statistics (NCES). The left chart displays average family income levels grouped by parent education and student educational expectations. Each cluster of bars represents a parent education level, with individual bars showing different student aspiration categories. The right chart shows the percentage of students expecting STEM careers at age 30, grouped by school location (City, Suburb, Town, Rural) and gender (Male, Female).

Key Findings

The visualization reveals a pronounced income gradient across parental education levels that reflects the fundamental mechanisms of intergenerational advantage transmission described in Bourdieu's cultural capital theory. Families where parents hold doctoral or professional degrees report average incomes approximately five to six times higher than families where parents lack a high school diploma. This disparity emerges from multiple reinforcing processes: credentialed parents access higher paying occupations in the primary labor market, accumulate wealth over time that compounds through investment returns, and transmit financial literacy and economic decision making skills to their children. The income returns to each additional level of education are nonlinear, with the steepest gains occurring at the transition from secondary to postsecondary credentials. Associate's degree holders earn roughly double the income of those with only high school completion, while Bachelor's degree holders earn substantially more than Associate's degree holders. These patterns demonstrate how educational attainment functions as a primary sorting mechanism for economic opportunity in contemporary society.

Within each parental education category, students with the highest educational aspirations consistently come from families with the highest incomes, revealing how economic resources and academic expectations develop in tandem through processes of status socialization. This pattern aligns with expectancy value theory, which posits that students calibrate their aspirations based on perceived likelihood of success and the value placed on achievement in their immediate social context. Children from affluent households observe parents who succeeded through educational pathways, encounter extended family members and social network contacts with advanced degrees, and receive messaging that frames graduate education as a normal and achievable life trajectory. Conversely, students from lower income households within each parental education stratum may perceive greater barriers to advanced education, including direct costs, opportunity costs, and cultural distance from academic institutions. The tight coupling between family income and student expectations within education categories suggests that economic resources provide advantages beyond those conferred by parental credentials alone.

The gender patterns in STEM career expectations challenge stereotypical assumptions about female underrepresentation in science and technology fields. Female students report higher rates of STEM career expectations than male students across all school locale types, with the overall female rate exceeding the male rate by approximately 15 percentage points. This finding may reflect recent cohort effects from sustained efforts to recruit women into STEM, changes in how students interpret STEM occupation categories, or sampling variations in how career expectations questions are framed. The International Standard Classification of Education provides harmonized codes that enable cross country comparison by grouping diverse national qualifications into equivalent levels. The mapping collapsed the eleven original codes into six broader categories: code zero became No Education; codes one and two became Primary; codes three and four became Lower Secondary; codes five and six became Upper Secondary; codes seven and eight became Post Secondary non tertiary; and codes nine and ten became Bachelor's or higher. This six category structure preserves key credential thresholds while reducing visual complexity. Records with missing or unknown education values were excluded to prevent undefined categories from appearing in the visualization. The category labels communicate educational attainment in accessible terms that do not require familiarity with ISCED coding schemes.

The continuous ESCS socioeconomic status index was transformed into tercile categories using cutpoints that divide the distribution into three groups of approximately equal size. Tercile rather than quartile categorization was selected to reduce the number of visual elements while maintaining sufficient granularity to detect SES effects. The bottom third of the distribution received the Low SES label, the middle third received Medium SES, and the top third received High SES. This categorization converts the continuous socioeconomic measure into an ordered categorical variable suitable for discrete color encoding, while preserving information about relative rank position within the distribution. The tercile transformation assumes that meaningful SES effects operate across broad economic strata rather than requiring fine grained distinctions, an assumption that prior research on SES achievement relationships supports.

Mathematics anxiety scores underwent mean aggregation within each combination of parental education level and SES tercile, transforming individual student records into eighteen group means that appear in the left chart. This aggregation serves visualization purposes by reducing overplotting and revealing systematic patterns, but it entails information loss. Within group variation disappears entirely, and outliers are absorbed into means that may not represent typical experiences. Student counts were preserved for tooltip display, enabling viewers to assess estimate stability based on sample sizes. The aggregation was implemented using pandas groupby with mean aggregation, producing the summary statistics that drive the circle position encoding.

The right chart displays a random sample of two thousand students selected without replacement from the full PISA dataset. This sampling addresses the overplotting challenge that would arise from displaying hundreds of thousands of points simultaneously, which would obscure patterns through point overlap. The two thousand point sample size balances visual legibility against statistical reliability: smaller samples might produce unstable patterns, while larger samples would recreate overplotting problems. Regression lines were computed on the sampled data using Altair's transform_regression function, which fits ordinary least squares models within each gender group. The regression computation assumes linear relationships between belonging and anxiety, an assumption that the scatter pattern should be examined to verify. The grouped regression produces separate slope and intercept estimates for females and males, enabling the visual comparison of gender specific trends.

Description

This visualization presents two linked charts examining geographic variation in academic achievement and gender based performance differences across subject domains. Key Findings

The left chart reveals substantial variation in mathematics achievement across continental regions that reflects long documented patterns in international educational assessment. Asian countries demonstrate the highest mean mathematics scores, with regional averages exceeding five hundred points on the PISA scale. This Asian achievement advantage has been attributed to multiple intersecting factors: intensive educational systems with extended instructional time and demanding mathematics curricula; cultural values that emphasize academic effort over innate ability, creating growth mindset orientations; high stakes examination systems that motivate student engagement throughout secondary education; and relatively equitable resource distribution within national educational systems that produces higher floor performance. European countries follow with above average performance levels, benefiting from established educational infrastructure, well prepared teacher workforces, and educational policy coordination through the Bologna Process. North American achievement falls near the international average despite substantial per pupil expenditure, potentially reflecting high internal inequality that produces averaged results masking both high achieving and low achieving subpopulations. African regions show the lowest mean performance, reflecting underinvestment in educational infrastructure, high rates of poverty that constrain learning opportunities, teacher shortages and inadequate training, and curricular misalignment with PISA assessment constructs. These regional patterns should be interpreted cautiously given that PISA participation varies across assessment cycles.

The right chart reveals that female students consistently outperform males in reading across all continental regions, demonstrating one of the most robust gender patterns in international educational assessment. Reading bars for females extend further than male reading bars in every regional comparison, with gaps typically ranging from twenty to forty points on the PISA scale. This magnitude represents approximately half a year to one year of typical learning progress, indicating that gender reading gaps are educationally meaningful rather than trivial statistical artifacts. The female reading advantage likely emerges from multiple developmental and socialization processes: girls demonstrate earlier language acquisition and stronger verbal skills from early childhood; they report higher rates of recreational reading that builds comprehension skills outside formal instruction; and they may receive instructional content that better engages their interests and learning preferences. The consistency of this pattern across culturally diverse regions suggests that female verbal advantages reflect widespread developmental and socialization processes rather than region specific factors. The persistence of these gaps despite varied educational policies indicates that closing gender reading gaps may require targeted interventions beyond standard curricular approaches.

Gender differences in mathematics show considerably more regional variation than reading gaps, indicating that mathematics gender patterns are more responsive to cultural and policy contexts. In some regions, males demonstrate modest advantages of five to fifteen points, while in others the gap narrows substantially or even reverses with females outperforming males. This variation suggests that mathematics gender gaps are more malleable than reading gaps and may be amenable to targeted intervention. The larger and more variable mathematics gaps in Asian regions may reflect traditional gender role expectations combined with intensive educational environments that amplify performance differences under high stakes conditions. European regions with smaller mathematics gaps may have implemented effective policies to engage girls in STEM education or may have cultural norms that assign less gendered meaning to mathematical competence. Science achievement shows the smallest gender gaps across most regions, potentially because science draws on both verbal and quantitative skills where male and female advantages partially offset each other.

Within gender groups, the relative ranking of academic domains remains fairly consistent across regional contexts, revealing systematic patterns in how males and females perform across different skill areas. Reading scores tend to be highest for female students regardless of regional context, while mathematics scores are often highest for male students. Science performance typically falls between mathematics and reading for both genders. This consistency suggests that domain specific gender patterns reflect widespread socialization processes that operate similarly across diverse cultures, even as overall achievement levels vary substantially by region. The positive covariation among domains within regions indicates that factors influencing achievement operate broadly rather than domain specifically: regions with high mathematics scores also tend to have high reading and science scores, suggesting common underlying determinants such as educational quality, resource allocation, and societal valuation of education. However, the distinct gender patterns within and across domains indicate that some factors do operate domain specifically, particularly those related to gender socialization and the development of domain specific identities and interests.

Data Transformations

Achievement scores for each academic domain were computed by averaging all available plausible values from PV1 through PV10 for mathematics, science, and reading. PISA methodology generates ten plausible values per student per domain to account for measurement uncertainty in achievement estimates. Each plausible value represents a draw from the posterior distribution of student ability given their item responses, and proper variance estimation requires combining results across all ten values. For visualization purposes, averaging the plausible values produces point estimates that align with the conditional means that PISA methodology targets. Using single plausible values rather than all ten would introduce unnecessary sampling variability, while the full variance estimation procedures required for statistical inference are not necessary for descriptive visualization. The averaging approach follows PISA technical documentation recommendations for producing summary statistics when inferential precision is not the primary concern.

Country to continent mapping aggregates approximately eighty PISA participating countries into seven regional groups using a predefined dictionary that assigns each three letter country code to a continental label. This transformation reduces the complexity of displaying country level data while enabling geographic pattern detection at a tractable grain. The mapping follows conventional geographic boundaries, though some assignment decisions involve judgment calls for countries that span multiple regions or have ambiguous geographic placement. Countries not included in the mapping were assigned to an Other category that serves as a residual group. This aggregation assumes sufficient within region coherence to justify pooling, an assumption that holds better for some regions than others. European countries share substantial educational policy coordination, while African or Asian countries span diverse educational and economic contexts that regional aggregation may obscure.

The data underwent reshaping from wide format, where mathematics, science, and reading scores occupied separate columns, to long format suitable for grouped bar visualization. This transformation using pandas melt or equivalent operations created a single score column paired with a subject domain indicator. A composite category variable was then constructed by concatenating gender labels with subject labels, producing the six categories (Female Math, Female Science, Female Reading, Male Math, Male Science, Male Reading) that structure the y axis of the right chart. This category construction enables the factorial display of gender by subject means within a single bar chart, avoiding the need for small multiples or faceting that would separate related comparisons.

Regional means were computed through a two stage aggregation pipeline that accounts for the hierarchical structure of international survey data. First, country level means were calculated by averaging student scores within each country, gender, and subject combination. Then continent level means were computed by averaging across countries within each region, with implicit equal weighting of countries regardless of sample size. This approach treats each country as the unit of analysis for regional aggregation, preventing populous countries from dominating regional estimates. Alternative approaches that weight by student count would produce different estimates that reflect the population weighted regional mean rather than the typical country experience. The transform_aggregate function in Altair implements this aggregation within the visualization specification.

Key Findings

The left chart reveals a strong positive relationship between school belonging and academic achievement across all three assessed domains that aligns with decades of research on the importance of school climate for student outcomes. Students reporting high school belonging, indicating that they feel accepted, respected, and connected to their school community, score approximately thirty to fifty points higher on average than students reporting low belonging. This magnitude represents roughly half a year of typical learning progress, indicating that belonging effects are educationally meaningful rather than trivial. The consistency of this pattern across mathematics, reading, and science suggests that belonging operates as a general support for academic engagement rather than affecting specific skill domains. Students who feel they belong may approach learning with less anxiety and greater willingness to take intellectual risks. They may have stronger relationships with teachers that facilitate help seeking and feedback utilization. They may attend school more regularly and participate more actively in classroom activities. These mechanisms collectively produce the achievement advantages visible in the belonging gradient.

Within each belonging level, the relative ranking of academic domains remains fairly consistent, with reading scores tending highest followed by science and mathematics. This domain ordering reflects patterns observed in other visualizations where reading achievement consistently exceeds mathematics achievement in the PISA sample. The parallel trajectories of the three domain lines across belonging levels indicate that belonging effects are approximately equal magnitude across subjects. This parallelism suggests that belonging operates through domain general mechanisms such as attendance, engagement, and teacher relationships rather than through domain specific pathways that would produce differential effects. The absence of belonging by domain interactions simplifies intervention design: programs that enhance school belonging can be expected to produce benefits across all academic areas without requiring subject specific modifications. The right scatter plot reveals a positive correlation between mathematics self efficacy and mathematics persistence that provides evidence for the social cognitive theory of motivation. This association likely reflects reciprocal causation: confidence encourages persistence, while successful persistence experiences build confidence. The regression slopes indicate that each standard deviation increase in self efficacy corresponds to approximately half a standard deviation increase in persistence, a meaningful association that underscores the interconnection of motivational constructs. The scatter pattern shows substantial variation around the regression lines, indicating that self efficacy explains only a portion of persistence differences and that other factors also contribute to students' willingness to engage with difficult content. The bivariate relationship provides a foundation for interventions that target either efficacy or persistence with the expectation of spillover effects on the other construct.

Immigration status moderates the self efficacy and persistence relationship in ways that illuminate immigrant integration processes in educational contexts. Native students show the densest concentration of points with the broadest distribution across both efficacy and persistence dimensions, reflecting their majority representation in the sample and the wide range of experiences within the native population. The immigrant paradox literature has documented high motivation among recent immigrants who view education as a pathway to upward mobility despite facing structural disadvantages. Second generation students display intermediate patterns, with efficacy and persistence distributions falling between native and first generation peers. This intermediate positioning suggests partial integration whereby second generation students have acquired some advantages of native familiarity while potentially losing some of the striving orientation that characterized their parents' generation. The variation in immigration patterns across belonging levels, accessible through the interaction, reveals whether school climate differentially affects immigrant versus native student mindsets.
